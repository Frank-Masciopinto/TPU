Q:I want to build a car hauler trailer. What width of trailer frame would i need and what axles? 


A: 82” wide frame is typical for a car hauler trailer. People put anything from 3.5k axles to 8k axles on car haulers. The 95” hub face 80” spring center is typically what people use for 3.5k - 7k axles and on a 8k axle they will use a 97” hub face with 80” spring center. These are the widest road legal axles you can use for your car hauler trailers.

Q: how do i know if i installed my trailer brake axle the right way so the brakes are not backwards?
A: the easiest way to to know you installed your trailer brake axle correctly is to make sure that the wires that are coming out of the brake axle are facing towards the back of the trailer. Additoally at times, there will be little stickers on the backingplates of the trailer brake axle that say left and right. The left side is the driver side and the right side is the passenger side (just like your driving your truck) these stickers are not always on the backing plates so make sure your brake wires are facing towards the back of the trailer and you should be good to go

Q: when lookin at your axle options i see you have hf/sc options but some of the sc options have pads loose? What does this mean?
A: the spring pads come loose so you can weld them where you want or we can also weld them where you want them its just a extra charge to do so. When the spring pads are pre welded in place then this makes them for exactly that width of trailer, however if you have a uncommon trailer width pads loose might be the best option for you. It provides more flexibility so you can actually weld the spring pads or spring perches or spring seats (different people call them different things) where you need them. The spring pads are what the springs sit on. The springs then hook onto the welded on hangers on your trailer. Since they hangers are already welded onto your trailer, so of the options that we have that have the spring pads pre welded on might not work for you, thats why we also offer spring pads loose.

Q: Will a st225/75r15 work in place of a 235/75r15 
A: YES, an ST225/75R15 tire can often work in place of a 235/75R15 tire


Understanding Tire Sizes
* 225 or 235: This number represents the tire's width in millimeters across the tread.
* 75: This is the aspect ratio. It's the percentage of the tire's height compared to its width. In this case, the sidewall height is 75% of the tire's width.
* R: Stands for radial construction.
* 15: The diameter of the wheel rim in inches.
Key Differences
* Width: The 235/75R15 tire is 10 millimeters wider than the ST225/75R15 tire. This wider tread generally means slightly better traction and stability.

* Overall Diameter: Since both tires have the same aspect ratio and rim diameter, their overall diameters will be slightly different. The 235/75R15 tire will be marginally taller. This can sometimes lead to a small speedometer inaccuracy.

Practical Implications
   * Fitment: Make sure the wider 235/75R15 tire can fit into your wheel wells without rubbing, especially at full steering lock or suspension compression.
   * Load Capacity: Trailer tires (ST) normally have a slightly higher load capacity compared to similarly sized light truck tires. Check the specific tire's ratings.

Q: WHATS A STRONGER BOLT THE GRADE 5 OR THE GRADE 8 BOLT?
A: Grade 8 bolts are generally better in terms of strength compared to Grade 5. Here’s a breakdown of the key differences:
   * Strength:
   * Grade 5 bolts have a tensile strength of about 120,000 psi.
   * Grade 8 bolts have a tensile strength of about 150,000 psi, making them stronger and better suited for high-stress applications.
   * Applications:
   * Grade 5 is commonly used in automotive applications, general-purpose machinery, and other medium-strength needs.
   * Grade 8 bolts are typically used in heavy-duty applications like construction equipment, heavy trailers, or where high strength is critical.
If you need higher strength and durability, especially for trailer parts, heavy machinery, or critical connections, Grade 8 would be the better choice. For more general use where extreme strength isn’t required, Grade 5 might be sufficient.

Below is a list of all known potential causes for caliper locking or dragging that may help in diagnosing the problem:
________________


1. Improper or Incompatible Brake Actuator (Pump)
Using a drum-rated or undersized hydraulic pump on a disc brake setup can cause pressure imbalance or failure to fully release pressure after braking. Some pumps maintain residual pressure, especially if they’re not rated for dual 12K disc axle setups.
2. Brake Line Routing or Length Differences
If the brake lines to the right side are longer or routed inefficiently (sharp bends, tight loops), it can cause delays in fluid pressure release and create imbalance between sides.
3. Trapped Air in the Brake Lines
Air pockets on one side can affect brake release timing and cause dragging. Always bleed starting from the farthest caliper from the actuator and work inward.
4. Brake Fluid Contamination
Old, dirty, or moisture-contaminated brake fluid can expand under heat and cause pressure buildup even without pedal input.
5. Collapsed Brake Hose (Internally)
A brake hose might look fine externally but act like a one-way valve internally — allowing pressure in but not releasing it completely.
________________


We’re happy to help troubleshoot further and work with you on resolving this. But from everything you've described, and the fact that both axles are only having issues on the right side, we strongly suspect something in the trailer's brake system setup — particularly the actuator or hydraulic routing — is the root cause.
It’s highly unlikely for two axles to exhibit the same defect, on the same trailer, and only on one side. Let us know how you'd like to proceed.

Q: WHY WOULD MY TRAILER BE SHAKING AND VIBRATING AT 55 MPH
A: if your trailer is shaking or vibrating at around 55 MPH, the most common reasons are:
________________


🔧 1. Tire/Wheel Balance Issues
      * Tires or wheels not balanced properly.

      * Missing wheel weights.

      * Flat spots on tires from sitting too long.

🔧 2. Bad or Worn Tires
         * Separated tire belts (inside the tire) can cause nasty vibrations.

         * Out-of-round tires (defective or old).

🔧 3. Hub or Bearing Problems
            * Worn-out bearings causing looseness and vibration.

            * Bent hubs or improperly torqued lug nuts.

🔧 4. Axle Problems
               * Bent axle spindle or entire axle.

               * Loose U-bolts holding the axle to the springs.

🔧 5. Suspension Issues
                  * Worn leaf spring bushings or shackles.

                  * Broken or loose hangers.

🔧 6. Load Distribution
                     * If the trailer isn't loaded properly (too much weight behind the axles), it can cause sway and shake.

                     * Tongue weight should generally be around 10–15% of total trailer weight.




________________


Here’s how to diagnose fast:
                        * First check: Tire pressure, tire condition, wheel balance.

                        * Second check: Feel the hubs after a drive — if they’re hot, bearing problem.

                        * Third check: Look for anything bent under the trailer — axles, springs, brackets.

                        * Fourth check: Jack up and spin each tire — look for wobble or roughness.

________________


My opinion:
In 80%+ of cases like this, it’s bad tires or bad balance, OR Lose hub — especially if the shaking comes in at a specific speed like 50–60 MPH.

Thinking about swapping your 7K axles for 8Ks?
Here’s one of the most common questions we get from customers:
“Do I have to change my wheels too?”
Not necessarily — but we recommend paying close attention to your tire rating to make sure your setup is properly rated for the increased axle capacity.
Yes the bolt pattern is the same for 7k and 8k axles, but the load rating and components matter if you're trying to do it right.
Same Bolt Pattern – But Load Rating Still Matters
Both 7K and 8K axles use the 8-lug on 6.5” bolt pattern, meaning you can reuse your 16” or 17.5” wheels on either axle.
However, you’ll want to check that:
                           * Your tires are rated to handle the load

                           * You’re using the right lug nuts for the wheels being installed

Just because it fits doesn’t mean it’s suited for the weight you're carrying.
Tire & Wheel Load Ratings — What You NEED to Know
Even if the bolt pattern fits, pay attention to your tire load rating. If your tires can’t handle the weight, upgrading the axle becomes pointless — your trailer will still be rated at the lower component.

You don’t have to change your wheels when upgrading from 7K to 8K axles —
but it’s smart to review your tire load rating, stud size (if running 17.5” on a 7K), and make sure the lug nuts match the wheels.
It’s all about keeping the setup strong, safe, and reliable — not just "fitting" it together.

Q:  hello i just purchased new carter trailer axles, do you have a maintenance schedule for them like when to put oil int hem and what not?


A: Trailer axle maintenance requires checking bearings and grease every 6–12 months or 3,000–12,000 miles, with more frequent service for heavy use or marine environments. Key tasks include cleaning/repacking bearings, checking brakes, and torqueing lug nuts. Annual, comprehensive inspections are essential for safety. 
Core Maintenance Schedule
                                       * Monthly/Before Each Trip: Check tire pressure, tire wear, and lug nut torque.
                                       * Every 3 Months/3,000 Miles (Heavy Use): Inspect brakes, grease hubs, and check for worn suspension parts.
                                       * Every 6 Months/6,000 Miles: Inspect axle beam for damage, check for loose bolts, and check brake magnets/shoes.
                                       * Annually/12,000 Miles (Standard Industry Recommendation): Fully repack wheel bearings, inspect bearing condition (replace if corroded or pitted), replace grease seals, and adjust brakes. 
Critical Inspection Points
                                       * Bearing Lubrication: Use a high-quality grease and avoid mixing different types.
                                       * Boat Trailers: Submersible trailers require more frequent lubrication, sometimes after every trip, to prevent water-induced corrosion.
                                       * Brakes: Test for responsiveness; replace worn pads/magnets.
                                       * Suspension: Inspect for rust, cracks, or worn bushings. 
For newly purchased trailers, it is recommended to check lug nuts and suspension bolts after the first 50 miles of use.

Equalizer/Center Bolts (7/8" or 1" bolts): 30–50 ft-lbs (ensure components do not bind).

U-Bolts (Axle to Springs): 35–90 ft-lbs (depends on diameter).
Safety Note: If using wet bolts or standard nuts, ensure they are tight but allow for suspension movement. If they are locking nuts, they should be tight against the hanger.
